Utilizes

What an odd word to behold.

Command of language is something that I have a lot of trouble with, at least when giving presentations or just speaking in general. It was very odd to a number of my peers that even when no script was demanded for a certain presentation, I still wrote the script and read it aloud again and again, knowing that spoken words were permanent but virtual ink on pixels was temporary. I would cozy up inside the backspace button, circle around it, and gaze upon what seemed like the panacea to my verbal qualms.

Evidently, I can’t do this when standing in front of others, so I have a small quick-fix for that. If I want to converse with someone for a while, I try to lead them into a set conversation that I’ve written out in my head tens and hundreds of times. I’ll ask questions I already know the answers to. I’ll string together topics to get back into my verbal elysium. I have a remarkable affinity for forcing a one-on-one conversation to go a certain way.

Part of this was forged out of some sort of social anxiety. I need to do things in a very set and defined order, otherwise control leaps out of my hands and I am thrust into an unfamiliar and hostile world. I listen to the same music at the same time every day. Walking across the quad to physics last year, I would listen to the “Cavalier Song” à la University of Virginia, musing about how “once more our might has won the fight,” knowing that the fight was simply just to stay in control of my environment.

One of these aforementioned verbal safe spaces is the topic of anxiety. It’s something I’m pretty familiar with, so if I want to talk to someone, it’s a good place to get them to stay. But then, I get asked the following: “how do you have anxiety if you’re talking to me just fine?” In a way, this demonstrates that what I’ve been doing has worked - do you realize why we’re still talking in the first place? I’m not anxious in this space in my mind.

So, born out of this desire for command of language, out of my pencil sprung a litany of literary cliches. “Demonstrates that…” “shows an intense dichotomy between…” “it is worth noting that…” But the most ubiquitous of these was the word “utilizes.”

I was in a hurry and time was winding down on that essay. I was frantically searching my suitcase for something, just a word I could wear so that I could act formal, act dignified. That’s when I met Utilizes. It was beautiful, just sublime. More dignified than “use,” and just the right complexity.

Of course, wherever I went, Utilizes came along. When I was meeting professors for the first time, it was by my side. And of course, we loved going on dates in the place we first met – when the time was winding down on an in-class essay.

Unfortunately, being in this relationship had its toll. I had begun to - and pardon me for bringing it here too - overutilize Utilizes. It was sad to see the panacea I had leaned on for so long become a crutch. I still loved Utilizes - a brilliant word - but my peers did not. “Why do you use it so much? You don’t sound like a human.” My US History teacher certainly believed that this word made me something inhuman, a machine built to spew out formalities in the most meager of situations.

But alas, I cannot give up this love. It represents everything that I admire - control over my own language, the formality that I adore - yet to be seen with Utilizes is to make me less than myself, a contraption of unfeeling steel, tapping at the keys to please someone else. With so much formality, yet no dignity at all – “Utilizes” is in the worst of both worlds.
